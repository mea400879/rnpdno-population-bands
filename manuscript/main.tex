% ---- 2.2 Methods -------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Spatial Weights}
\label{sec:weights}

We construct a Queen contiguity spatial weights matrix for the 2,478 valid
municipalities using the \texttt{libpysal} library \citep{Rey2021}.
The matrix is row-standardised.
Of the 2,478 municipalities, 52 are islands (no Queen neighbours); these are
retained in the analysis, as the conditional-permutation approach
handles islands by assigning them a pseudo-\textit{p}-value based on the
empirical distribution under random relabelling \citep{Anselin1995}.

\subsection{Global Spatial Autocorrelation}
\label{sec:global}

Global Moran's $I$ is computed as:

\begin{equation}
  I = \frac{n}{\sum_i \sum_j w_{ij}}
      \cdot
      \frac{\sum_i \sum_j w_{ij}(x_i - \bar{x})(x_j - \bar{x})}
           {\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2}
  \label{eq:moran}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $x_i$ is the case count in municipality $i$, $w_{ij}$ are
row-standardised Queen-contiguity weights, and $n = 2{,}478$.
Statistical significance is assessed via 999 conditional permutations;
$\alpha = 0.05$.

\subsection{Local Spatial Autocorrelation (LISA)}
\label{sec:lisa}

Local Moran's $I_i$ for municipality $i$ is:

\begin{equation}
  I_i = \frac{(x_i - \bar{x})}{m_2} \sum_j w_{ij}(x_j - \bar{x}), \quad
  m_2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2
  \label{eq:lisa}
\end{equation}

Municipalities are classified into five types: High-High (HH), Low-Low (LL),
High-Low (HL), Low-High (LH), and Not Significant (NS) at $\alpha = 0.05$
\citep{Anselin1995}.
HH clusters are connected components of spatially contiguous HH municipalities
with a minimum size of three municipalities, identified via breadth-first
search on the Queen adjacency graph.

\subsection{Geographic Concentration}
\label{sec:concentration}

We quantify geographic concentration using the Gini coefficient:

\begin{equation}
  G = \frac{2\sum_{i=1}^{n} i \cdot x_{(i)}}{n \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{(i)}}
      - \frac{n+1}{n}
  \label{eq:gini}
\end{equation}

\noindent and the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI):

\begin{equation}
  \text{HHI} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i^2, \quad s_i = \frac{x_i}{\sum_j x_j}
  \label{eq:hhi}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $x_{(i)}$ is the $i$-th order statistic of the count distribution.
Both metrics are computed for each of the 132 monthly cross-sections per status.

\subsection{Trend Analysis}
\label{sec:trend}

We fit OLS regressions of the form $y_t = \alpha + \beta t + \varepsilon_t$
separately for each (region, status) combination, where $y_t$ is the monthly
count of HH municipalities ($t = 1, \ldots, 132$).
Slopes are expressed as municipalities per year ($\hat\beta \times 12$).
Statistical significance assessed at $\alpha = 0.05$ using heteroskedasticity-
consistent standard errors.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Raw counts only.} No population normalisation. Concentration
        in urban/high-population municipalities may partly reflect population
        size rather than elevated risk. Population-normalised analysis reserved
        for Paper~2.
  \item \textbf{Ecological fallacy.} Municipal-level analysis cannot make
        individual-level inferences.
  \item \textbf{Zero-infilling assumption.} Municipality-months absent from the
        RNPDNO export are treated as zero cases. If absences reflect reporting
        failures rather than true zeros, concentration metrics are underestimated
        in under-reporting municipalities.
  \item \textbf{Spatial weights specification.} Queen contiguity may not capture
        functional connectivity (road networks, institutional jurisdictions).
        Sensitivity to alternative weight matrices is left for future work.
  \item \textbf{Descriptive design.} The analysis documents patterns; it does
        not identify causal mechanisms. All regional narratives are hypotheses.
  \item \textbf{Potential underreporting.} RNPDNO relies on official registry
        data. Disappearances in areas with weak state presence or high impunity
        may be systematically underreported, biasing hotspot maps toward
        high-capacity jurisdictions.
\end{enumerate}
