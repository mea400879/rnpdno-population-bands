\abstract{\noindent\textbf{Objectives.}
Spatial analyses of disappearances in Mexico have treated them as a monolithic phenomenon, yet the national registry distinguishes three outcome categories that reflect fundamentally different processes: active search failures (Not Located), living recoveries (Located Alive), and lethal outcomes (Located Dead). If these processes have different spatial drivers, composite maps conflating all outcomes will misallocate forensic and prevention resources. We present the first outcome-disaggregated spatial analysis, examining whether the three outcome categories and their aggregate (Total) produce distinct hotspot geographies at the national scale.

\noindent\textbf{Methods.}
We analyze \nmunis{} municipalities over \nmonths{} months (January 2015--December 2025) using Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) with Queen contiguity weights (\nLISAruns{} runs: 4 categories $\times$ 3 sexes $\times$ \nmonths{} months).
Geographic concentration is measured via Gini coefficients benchmarked against the law of crime concentration.
Cross-category spatial overlap is quantified using Jaccard similarity on pairwise High-High cluster sets.
Trend slopes use OLS with Newey-West HAC standard errors.

\noindent\textbf{Results.}
Outcome categories are spatially non-interchangeable: Jaccard similarity between Located Dead and Not Located hotspots is 0.143; between Located Dead and Located Alive, 0.151.
This divergence is not driven by population size, municipal populations of HH sets do not differ significantly across outcome statuses (Mann-Whitney $U$, all pairwise $p > 0.50$).
Concentration substantially exceeds the Weisburd benchmark: 50\% of Not Located cases concentrate in 42 municipalities (1.7\%), and 50\% of Located Dead in 22 (0.9\%).
Centro is the fastest-growing cluster region (Not Located: $+4.96$ municipalities/year, $p < 0.001$), a dynamic invisible to prior annual analyses.
A structural break in the Baj\'{i}o industrial corridor at February 2017 ($p < 0.001$) separates a declining phase from a post-2017 expansion in Not Located clustering ($+0.50$ municipalities/year, $p < 0.001$).
Sex disaggregation reveals divergent male/female hotspot geographies for Not Located (Jaccard = 0.262) and a statistical power failure for female Located Dead (zero HH municipalities).

\noindent\textbf{Conclusions.}
Forensic identification resources and active search programs require different geographic targeting: Located Dead hotspots and Not Located hotspots are fundamentally different places.}
