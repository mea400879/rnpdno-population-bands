\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents the first national-scale, outcome-disaggregated, monthly spatial analysis of disappearances in Mexico, covering \nmunis{} municipalities over \nmonths{} months across four outcome categories through \nLISAruns{} LISA runs.
Five empirical findings are established.
First, disappearance outcome categories are spatially non-interchangeable: Jaccard similarity between Located Dead and either Not Located ($0.143$) or Located Alive ($0.151$) confirms that lethal-outcome geography is distinct from the geography of active disappearances.
Second, geographic concentration is extreme, substantially exceeding established criminological benchmarks: 50\% of Not Located cases in \referencemonth{} concentrate in 42 municipalities (1.7\%), and 50\% of Located Dead in 22 (0.9\%).
Third, Centro is the fastest-growing hotspot region, with Not Located HH municipalities expanding at $+4.96$/yr ($p < 0.001$), a dynamic invisible to prior annual analyses.
Fourth, the Baj\'{i}o industrial corridor exhibits a structural break at February 2017: prior to the break, HH municipalities within the 29-municipality corridor were declining ($\beta_1 = -8.82$/yr), while the post-2017 period shows a slow but statistically significant expansion ($\beta_2 = +0.47$/yr, $p = 0.046$) driven primarily by Not Located cases ($+0.50$/yr, $p < 0.001$). Fifth, external validation against the Violence Against Women Risk Index confirms that HH municipalities coincide with areas of elevated recorded violence regardless of victim sex, with rank-biserial correlations of 0.76 to 0.96 across all sex and status combinations, indicating that the LISA clusters capture genuine violence concentrations rather than statistical artifacts.


These findings rest on raw counts without population normalization, on a registry that captures registrations rather than verified events, and on a descriptive design that cannot identify causal mechanisms.
Subject to these caveats, one operational implication follows: forensic identification resources should follow Located Dead hotspot geography; active search programs should follow Not Located hotspot geography.
These are different places.
A rate-based companion analysis incorporating population normalization is forthcoming.
