\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents the first national-scale, outcome-disaggregated, monthly spatial analysis of disappearances in Mexico, covering \nmunis{} municipalities over \nmonths{} months through \nLISAruns{} LISA runs.

Four empirical facts are established.
First, disappearance outcome statuses are spatially non-interchangeable: Jaccard similarity between Located Dead and either Not Located ($0.143$) or Located Alive ($0.151$) confirms that lethal-outcome geography is distinct from the geography of active disappearances.
Second, geographic concentration is extreme---50\% of Not Located cases in \referencemonth{} concentrate in 42 municipalities (1.7\%), and 50\% of Located Dead in 22 (0.9\%).
Third, Centro is the fastest-growing hotspot region, with Not Located HH municipalities expanding at $+4.96$/yr ($p < 0.001$).
Fourth, the ``rising Baj\'{i}o'' hypothesis receives no support: Total HH municipalities declined ($-1.09$/yr, $p = 0.024$) and no sub-status shows a significant trend.

These findings rest on raw counts without population normalization, on a registry that captures registrations rather than verified events, and on a descriptive design that cannot identify causal mechanisms.
Subject to these caveats, the policy implication is direct: forensic identification resources should follow Located Dead hotspot geography; active search programs should follow Not Located hotspot geography.
These are different places.

A companion paper will test the robustness of the non-interchangeability result under population-normalized rates; a subsequent econometric study will incorporate external covariates to move from spatial description toward causal inference.
