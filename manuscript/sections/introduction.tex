\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% ---- Block 1: The data paradox ------------------------------------------------
The \textit{Registro Nacional de Personas Desaparecidas y No Localizadas} (RNPDNO) documents over 132,000 persons whose whereabouts remain unknown in Mexico, a figure widely acknowledged to undercount the true scale \citep{guercke_2025}.
In April 2026, the UN Committee on Enforced Disappearances (CED) issued its first referral to the General Assembly under Article~34 of the Convention, finding that enforced disappearances in Mexico constitute crimes against humanity occurring at different moments and in different parts of the territory \citep{CED_MEX_art34_2026}.
Yet despite the scale and geographic breadth of the crisis, no national-level spatial analysis has disaggregated disappearances by outcome status.
The RNPDNO distinguishes three outcomes (Not Located, Located Alive, Located Dead) that reflect fundamentally different processes: active search failures, living recoveries, and lethal outcomes \cite{cruz-santiago_lists_2020}.
If these processes have different spatial drivers, composite maps conflating all outcomes will misallocate forensic and prevention resources.

% ---- Block 2: Literature review ------------------------------------------------
Spatial analysis of violence in Mexico has focused almost exclusively on homicides, leaving disappearances either unexamined or treated as a residual category.
\citet{vilalta_muggah_2016, vilalta_2021} modeled homicide clustering in Mexico City at the neighborhood level.
\citet{rios_2013} linked cartel territorial competition to homicide surges nationally.
\citet{prieto_curiel_2023} modeled cartel population dynamics and 
estimated that Mexican cartels employ 160,000 to 185,000 members, 
sustained by recruiting 350 to 370 persons per week. \citet{cadena_garrocho_2019} produced the first national-scale municipal 
analysis to include disappearances alongside homicides for 2006 to 2017, 
applying LISA clustering to all 2,458 municipalities, but used cumulative 
totals over the full period and could not disaggregate disappearance 
outcomes because the predecessor registry (RNPED) did not distinguish 
them.
\citet{baptista_davila_2026} applied spatial zero-inflated Poisson models to municipal homicide mortality, confronting the same small-area instability that affects disappearance counts, but did not extend the framework to disappearances.
\citet{das_2025} demonstrated spatial spillover of organized crime violence into public health outcomes, and \citet{atuesta_2024} documented disappearance patterns linked to trafficking routes in three northern states, though without outcome decomposition.
At the single-city scale, \citet{aguilar_velazquez_2025} applied data-driven methods to disappearances in Mexico City.
At the subnational measurement level, \citet{escobar_vawri_2026} 
demonstrated that adaptive Empirical Bayes smoothing calibrated to 
population size yields reliable municipal-level estimates from 
zero-inflated violence records in Mexico.
The same small-area instability that distorts gender-based violence 
indices also affects disappearance counts, particularly for the 
Located Dead category where monthly municipal observations are 
overwhelmingly zero. That methodological insight is directly relevant here, as the same zero-inflation and population heterogeneity that distort municipal violence rates also affect disappearance counts.
None of these studies, however, disaggregated disappearances by outcome or examined the full national territory at monthly resolution.


This gap is consequential.
The institutional dimension underscores the concern: between 2006 and 2017, only 19 of 732 disappearance investigations reached court, with just 9 convictions \citep{garcia_castillo_2024, guevara_bermudez_2018}, indicating that case resolution is not merely rare but geographically variable in ways that aggregate analysis cannot capture.
From a routine activities perspective \citep{cohen_felson_1979}, the opportunity structures generating disappearances need not coincide with the institutional and geographic conditions under which cases are resolved or bodies recovered.
A municipality that is a hotspot for living recoveries may not be a hotspot for lethal outcomes; a municipality with many unresolved cases may not be where remains are found.
Forensic identification teams should be deployed where lethal outcomes concentrate, while active search programs should target areas of concentrated unresolved cases.
These may be different places, but without outcome-disaggregated analysis, we cannot know.

% ---- Block 4: Contribution -----------------------------------------------------
This paper presents the first outcome-disaggregated spatial analysis of disappearances in Mexico at the national scale with monthly resolution.
We analyze \nmunis{} municipalities over \nmonths{} months (\studyperiod{}) across three outcome categories and their aggregate, executing \nLISAruns{} LISA runs.
Two analytical contributions distinguish this work.
First, the outcome-disaggregation framework reveals that different outcome categories produce non-overlapping hotspot geographies, quantified through Jaccard similarity coefficients on pairwise HH cluster sets.
Second, the monthly temporal resolution reveals dynamics invisible to annual analyses, most notably the rapid expansion of HH clusters in Mexico's Centro region beginning around 2019 and a structural break in the Baj\'{i}o industrial corridor at February 2017.

% ---- Block 5: Research questions -----------------------------------------------
This paper addresses four research questions (RQs):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[RQ1] How geographically concentrated are
        disappearances, and how does concentration differ across outcome categories?
  \item[RQ2] To what extent do the three outcome categories 
        produce distinct spatial cluster geographies at the municipal level, 
        and how large is the divergence?
  \item [RQ3] How has the spatial distribution of
        each category evolved from 2015 to 2025, and where do regional hotspot 
        trajectories diverge by outcome?
  \item [RQ4] How do male and female disappearances
        differ in their spatial clustering across outcome categories?
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The alignment between our empirical findings and the CED's geographic characterization of disappearance patterns across eight named states is examined as a contextual application in Section~\ref{sec:ced}.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data sources and regional classification; Section~\ref{sec:methods} presents the spatial and statistical methods; Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results for each research question; Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and limitations; and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
